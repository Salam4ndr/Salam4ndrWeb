How Does the Caesar Cipher Work?

The Caesar Cipher is one of the oldest encryption methods. It is named after Julius Caesar, who used it to securely communicate with his generals. It is a substitution cipher, which means that each letter in the message is replaced with another letter a certain number of positions forward in the alphabet.

For example, if the chosen shift number is 3, the letter "A" becomes "D", "B" becomes "E", "C" becomes "F", and so on. When reaching the end of the alphabet, it wraps around back to "A". This method is very simple, but in the past, it was useful for encrypting messages quickly and practically.

However, its security is very low. With just a frequency analysis, it is easy to determine the shift number and decrypt the message. Despite this, the Caesar Cipher is still used today as an introduction to encryption concepts in cryptography courses.
